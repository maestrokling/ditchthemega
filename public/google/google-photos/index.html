Google Photos

Your photo library — and what Google does with it

⚠️ The privacy case

Google uses photo content for its AI models and to improve its products. Facial recognition data is collected from your photos. In 2021, Google ended its free unlimited storage, requiring payment for additional storage — which also gave many users their first reason to reconsider the arrangement.

What it is

Google Photos offers 15GB free storage (shared with Gmail and Drive) and unlimited storage for photos backed up at "Storage Saver" quality. It includes AI-powered organization, facial recognition, search by content, and automatic albums. It's genuinely excellent software. It also gives Google access to every photo you've ever taken — including photos of your home, your family, your documents, your face.

What you lose

  • The best AI-powered photo organization available (genuinely hard to match)
  • Facial recognition and object search
  • Seamless Android integration
  • 15GB free storage

Honest assessment

Google Photos is arguably the best photo management software that exists. The alternatives are meaningfully worse at AI organization. iCloud Photos is close on Apple devices. Everything else involves tradeoffs in features, storage cost, or complexity. The privacy argument is strong; the convenience argument for staying is also strong. This is one where the honest answer is: it depends on how much the privacy tradeoff matters to you.

Data to export first

  • Google Takeout (takeout.google.com) → select Google Photos → export all albums
  • Large libraries may require multiple exports (Google splits into 2GB files)
  • Alternatively, download albums directly in Google Photos → select → three dots → Download

Alternatives

  • Apple iCloud Photos 5GB free; $0.99/month 50GB; $2.99/month 200GB — Best alternative for iPhone users. On-device processing for facial recognition (not sent to Apple's servers). Seamless iPhone integration. iCloud Shared Photo Library for families. Better privacy than Google; slightly weaker AI organization.
  • Proton Drive 1GB free; from $3.99/month 200GB — End-to-end encrypted. Photos stay private even from Proton. No AI organization — it's encrypted storage, not smart photo management. Best for people who prioritize privacy over features.
  • Immich (self-hosted) Free (you provide the hardware) — Open-source Google Photos alternative. Self-hosted on your own server or NAS. Facial recognition, album organization, mobile backup. Full Google Photos feature set with zero cloud dependency. Requires technical setup.
  • Synology Photos (NAS) One-time hardware ($200–400) + free software — Run your own photo server at home. Synology Photos includes facial recognition, albums, and mobile backup. One-time hardware cost, no ongoing subscription. Excellent for families wanting local control.
  • Amazon Photos Unlimited for Prime members; 5GB free otherwise — Trades Google's surveillance for Amazon's. Not a privacy win, but a consolidation option if you're already a Prime member. Not recommended if you're trying to leave both ecosystems.

Migration steps

  1. Export all photos via Google Takeout (allows organization by album)
  2. Choose your destination (iCloud for Apple users, Proton Drive for privacy, Immich for full control)
  3. Upload to your new home — most platforms have bulk import tools
  4. Set up automatic backup from your phone to the new service
  5. Verify backup is working correctly before deleting from Google Photos
  6. Delete photos from Google Photos after confirmed backup (Google Photos → Settings → Free up device storage won't help here — delete from Google's servers via takeout or manual deletion)
